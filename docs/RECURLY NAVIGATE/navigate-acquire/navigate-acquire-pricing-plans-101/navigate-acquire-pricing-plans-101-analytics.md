    <!-- Section: Six metrics -->
    <div class="rc-lp-section">
      <h2><i class="fa-solid fa-gauge-high rc-fa-section"></i> Six metrics to track from launch</h2>
      <p>These are the metrics most directly shaped by how you've set up your plans, pricing models, and trials. Review them within the first 30 days of launch and continue monitoring monthly.</p>

      <div class="rc-kpi-grid">

        <div class="rc-kpi-card">
          <div class="rc-kpi-card-top">
            <div class="rc-kpi-icon"><i class="fa-solid fa-dollar-sign"></i></div>
            <span class="rc-kpi-name">MRR</span>
          </div>
          <span class="rc-kpi-where">MRR by Plan</span>
          <p class="rc-kpi-def">Monthly Recurring Revenue — the predictable revenue your active subscriptions generate each month, normalized to a monthly figure regardless of billing interval.</p>
          <p class="rc-kpi-watch"><strong>Watch for:</strong> Which plans drive the most MRR. If annual plans represent a small share of MRR relative to subscriber count, your annual plan pricing or discounting may need adjustment.</p>
        </div>

        <div class="rc-kpi-card">
          <div class="rc-kpi-card-top">
            <div class="rc-kpi-icon"><i class="fa-solid fa-users"></i></div>
            <span class="rc-kpi-name">Active subscribers</span>
          </div>
          <span class="rc-kpi-where">Analytics Dashboard</span>
          <p class="rc-kpi-def">The count of unique subscribers with at least one active subscription. Note: a subscriber with multiple subscriptions is counted once, not once per subscription.</p>
          <p class="rc-kpi-watch"><strong>Watch for:</strong> Subscriber count growing faster or slower than MRR. Fast subscriber growth with slow MRR growth may signal that lower-priced plans are acquiring more than higher-value ones.</p>
        </div>

        <div class="rc-kpi-card">
          <div class="rc-kpi-card-top">
            <div class="rc-kpi-icon"><i class="fa-solid fa-arrow-right-from-bracket"></i></div>
            <span class="rc-kpi-name">Churn rate</span>
          </div>
          <span class="rc-kpi-where">Plans, Pricing &amp; Promotions</span>
          <p class="rc-kpi-def">The percentage of subscribers who cancelled in a given period relative to active subscribers at the start of that period. Available at the per-plan level in the Plans, Pricing &amp; Promotions dashboard.</p>
          <p class="rc-kpi-watch"><strong>Watch for:</strong> High churn on a specific plan shortly after the first charge. This often signals a pricing perception problem — subscribers don't feel the value matched the price when the trial ended.</p>
        </div>

        <div class="rc-kpi-card">
          <div class="rc-kpi-card-top">
            <div class="rc-kpi-icon"><i class="fa-solid fa-user-plus"></i></div>
            <span class="rc-kpi-name">Acquisition rate</span>
          </div>
          <span class="rc-kpi-where">Plans, Pricing &amp; Promotions</span>
          <p class="rc-kpi-def">The rate at which new subscribers are joining each plan. Tracked per plan so you can compare which plans are converting visitors into subscribers most effectively.</p>
          <p class="rc-kpi-watch"><strong>Watch for:</strong> One plan dramatically outperforming others. This is often your natural "anchor" plan — consider whether its pricing and packaging should be more prominent in your checkout experience.</p>
        </div>

        <div class="rc-kpi-card">
          <div class="rc-kpi-card-top">
            <div class="rc-kpi-icon"><i class="fa-solid fa-chart-bar"></i></div>
            <span class="rc-kpi-name">ARPS</span>
          </div>
          <span class="rc-kpi-where">Analytics Dashboard</span>
          <p class="rc-kpi-def">Average Revenue Per Subscriber — MRR at end of month divided by active subscribers at end of month. A simple measure of how much each subscriber contributes on average.</p>
          <p class="rc-kpi-watch"><strong>Watch for:</strong> ARPS trending down over time while subscriber count grows. This indicates newer subscribers are joining at lower-priced plans, which may be intentional or may signal your higher-value plans need better visibility.</p>
        </div>

        <div class="rc-kpi-card">
          <div class="rc-kpi-card-top">
            <div class="rc-kpi-icon"><i class="fa-solid fa-hourglass-half"></i></div>
            <span class="rc-kpi-name">Trial conversion rate</span>
          </div>
          <span class="rc-kpi-where">Plans, Pricing &amp; Promotions</span>
          <p class="rc-kpi-def">The percentage of trial subscribers who convert to paid at the end of their trial period. Tracked per plan so you can compare card-required vs. cardless trials directly.</p>
          <p class="rc-kpi-watch"><strong>Watch for:</strong> Conversion rates below 34% — the approximate industry average. If you're consistently below this, review your trial length, onboarding experience, and the 3-day pre-trial-end communication.</p>
        </div>

      </div>
    </div>

    <!-- Section: MRR growth breakout -->
    <div class="rc-lp-section">
      <h2><i class="fa-solid fa-magnifying-glass-chart rc-fa-section"></i> Reading the MRR growth breakout</h2>
      <p>The MRR by Plan dashboard breaks net MRR movement into five categories. Understanding each one helps you diagnose whether your pricing structure is working as intended.</p>

      <div class="rc-table-wrap">
        <table class="rc-table">
          <thead>
            <tr>
              <th>Category</th>
              <th>What it means</th>
              <th>What it tells you about your plans</th>
            </tr>
          </thead>
          <tbody>
            <tr>
              <td>New business MRR</td>
              <td>Revenue from subscribers joining for the first time</td>
              <td>Your acquisition plans and trial setup are working if this grows steadily</td>
            </tr>
            <tr>
              <td>Expansion MRR</td>
              <td>Revenue from existing subscribers upgrading or adding add-ons</td>
              <td>Your add-on architecture and plan upgrade path are generating incremental revenue</td>
            </tr>
            <tr>
              <td>Contraction MRR</td>
              <td>Revenue lost from existing subscribers downgrading</td>
              <td>Subscribers are stepping down to lower-priced plans — check whether your mid-tier plans are priced to retain</td>
            </tr>
            <tr>
              <td>Churn MRR</td>
              <td>Revenue lost from subscribers cancelling entirely</td>
              <td>The cost of voluntary and involuntary churn — the primary metric to monitor for pricing-driven cancellations</td>
            </tr>
            <tr>
              <td>Reactivation MRR</td>
              <td>Revenue from previously churned subscribers returning</td>
              <td>Former subscribers coming back — often a signal that win-back pricing or offers are working</td>
            </tr>
          </tbody>
        </table>
      </div>

      <div class="rc-accent-card rc-accent-yellow">
        <h4>A healthy growth picture</h4>
        <ul>
          <li><strong>New business and expansion MRR exceed contraction and churn MRR.</strong> Net MRR is positive — your plan architecture is generating more than it's losing.</li>
          <li><strong>Expansion MRR grows over time.</strong> Subscribers are finding more value and purchasing add-ons or upgrading — a direct signal that your add-on and plan structure is working.</li>
          <li><strong>Churn MRR is concentrated in lower-priced plans.</strong> Higher-value plans retain better; lower-priced plans churn more but are also easier to replace with new acquisition.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
